For most of my career I worked in spaces where being seen was part of the job. Not in a performative sense — just ambiently. You could glance up and clock who was deep in something, who was stuck, who was free for a quick question. Collaboration by proximity.

When I finally had a door to close, I thought I'd get more done. I did, eventually. But first I had to grieve a subtle thing: the background hum of other people making things. It turns out I'd been borrowing their momentum without knowing it.

## The first week

I kept the door open out of reflex. People walked by and I half-waved, half-didn't, which is a terrible compromise. I felt like I was in a staged office, waiting for my real job to start.

Then on Thursday I closed it. And I sat there, in an amount of quiet I hadn't experienced since before 2020, and I could hear my own thinking for the first time in a long time. It was not a pleasant sound at first. It was jittery and defensive and a little bit bored.

## What changed

The thing I didn't expect: my writing got better almost immediately. Not the sentences — the _thinking behind_ the sentences. When you're always slightly on-stage, you tend to write toward a legible version of yourself. With the door closed, I started to notice when I was performing clarity instead of finding it.

> Open plans optimize for the output of groups and the suffering of individuals. Closed doors optimize for the inverse. Neither is correct in isolation.

I've started leaving the door open mornings and closing it afternoons. This is almost certainly too neat a solution to hold up, but right now it feels like the right compromise — a little bit social, a little bit selfish, mostly honest.
