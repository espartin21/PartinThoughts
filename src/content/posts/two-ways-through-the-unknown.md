I've sat there, staring into the murky waters of the unknown — unsure of how to proceed, unsure of who to ask, unsure of myself.

## How I respond

When faced with unknowns, I've noticed that I usually have two recurring responses:

1. Unsure — paralyzed by fear
2. Headstrong assuredness — blindly moving forward

### Unsureness

When we are unsure, it can be hard to understand how to even go forward. We don't know what we don't know.

When my job started to fully adopt AI towards the end of 2025, I was nervous; nervous about how to use these new tools, nervous about what the future of my job looked like, and nervous that I wouldn't be able to adapt. The unknown had left me standing in the dark.

A few of my coworkers had become proficient with Claude Code by the end of the year. At the beginning of the new year, I spent time reading docs, blogs, and watching YouTube videos, yet I still felt lost. I still felt the unknown around me.

A coworker pointed me to a task he thought would be a good place to start. I asked if he would spend the day pair-programming with me and giving me oversight while I worked through the task. He happily agreed.

He spent the day holding my hand — giving me tips, things like starting each big task in "plan" mode.

The coworker shares the same name as my dad. At the end of the day, after shipping the task, I started to say something, then stopped myself. He asked what I was going to say. I replied, "This reminded me of working with my dad."

I still didn't know what I was doing, but I knew what to ask next.

### Headstrong assuredness

The other response is harder to admit, because it usually looks like confidence from the outside. It isn't, really. It's stubbornness with better posture.

I recently had to replace the front turn signals on my motorcycle — a task that the guy at the parts counter told me would be simple. I got my parts, my toolbox, and headed outside with the self-assuredness that I would be done in 20 minutes. In and out.

As I started trying to understand how the wiring ran through the bike, I realized it wasn't the easy job I'd been told. I searched my owner's manual, looked for YouTube videos, even service manuals — nothing would guide me and give me the instruction I needed.

Unwilling to spend the money to take my bike to the dealership for them to fix it, I dived head-first into the unknown. Armed with a few screwdrivers, I began to dismantle the front of my bike until I'd exposed enough wiring to see what connected to what.

A dozen screws later, two wires yanked, and plastic parts flexed back, I had the new turn signals in. Twenty minutes had become two hours. I flipped the switch and the signals blinked.

## So what does this mean?

Two different scenarios, two different approaches, two different solutions. Why was I scared in one situation, yet blindly confident in the other?

I'm unsure.

Was it because I grew up turning wrenches with my dad in the driveway? Was it because the guy at the parts counter told me it would be easy?

I'm unsure.

Did being unsure and relying on others keep me from initially learning beyond what my coworker could teach me? Did being headstrong risk damaging my bike by not knowing what I was doing?

I'm unsure.

Do I look to others to be guided and learn firsthand? Or do I go forward alone and find my own path?

I'm unsure...
